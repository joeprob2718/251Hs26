Introduction

Sometimes we get lucky and have an "easy" ODE that can be solved with simple antidifferentiation.

Some Special Cases of First-Order ODEs

The most general form of a first-order ODE is

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y).\]

The special case where the independent variable doesn't appear in the ODE,

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = f(y),\]

is called autonomous — the "law of nature" (the ODE) doesn't depend on the independent variable.

Instructor notes: Examples of autonomous ODEs include Newton's cooling (constant ambient temperature), Torricelli's law, and population models. These can be solved with separable-variable steps (Section 1.4) and analyzed with phase lines (Section 2.2).

For many phenomena, we first consider the simpler case with these "static laws of nature" since they're simpler to analyze, before considering a refined model of the form \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y)\).

1 We'll completely understand the qualitative behavior of any first-order autonomous ODE in Section 2.2.

Solving by Direct Integration

The "opposite" special case is

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x),\]

where the differential equation doesn't actually involve the unknown function itself.

While technically still a differential equation, it's "easy" in the sense that it can be solved for \(y(x)\) by simply antidifferentiating \(f(x)\):

\[y(x) = \int f(x)\, dx + C.\]

Example: First-Order Antiderivative Equation

Example

Consider \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = e^{2x} + x^2\).

  1. Find the general solution.
  2. Find the particular solution satisfying \(y(1) = 3\).
  3. Take a look on Desmos!

(a) General Solution

Integrate both sides with respect to \(x\):

\[y = \int \left(e^{2x} + x^2\right) dx\]
\[y = \frac{1}{2}\,e^{2x} + \frac{x^3}{3} + C\]

(b) Particular Solution with \(y(1) = 3\)

Apply the initial condition \(y(1) = 3\):

\[3 = \frac{1}{2}\,e^{2 \cdot 1} + \frac{1^3}{3} + C = \frac{1}{2}\,e^2 + \frac{1}{3} + C\]

Solving for \(C\):

\[C = 3 - \frac{1}{2}\,e^2 - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{8}{3} - \frac{1}{2}\,e^2\]

Therefore the particular solution is:

\[y = \frac{1}{2}\,e^{2x} + \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{8}{3} - \frac{1}{2}\,e^2\]

Higher-Order Antiderivative Equations

This "solvable by directly integrating" approach also works for equations of the following forms:

\[\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} = f(x), \qquad \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3} = f(x), \qquad \ldots, \qquad \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} = f(x).\]

We obtain one integration constant per derivative we undo, and so we obtain an n-parameter family for the nth-order "antiderivative equation".

Worked Example — 3rd-Order Equation

Solve \(y''' = e^{x/2} + 1\).

Step 1: Integrate once:

\[y'' = 2e^{x/2} + x + C_1\]

Step 2: Integrate again:

\[y' = 4e^{x/2} + \frac{x^2}{2} + C_1 x + C_2\]

Step 3: Integrate a third time:

\[y = 8e^{x/2} + \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{C_1}{2}\,x^2 + C_2\,x + C_3\]

This is a 3-parameter family of solutions (one constant per integration).

Position, Velocity, and Acceleration

We consider a particle moving along the \(x\)-axis with position/displacement given by

\[x = f(t).\]

Its velocity is

\[v = \frac{dx}{dt} = f'(t),\]

and its speed is \(|v|\). Its acceleration is

\[a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} = f''(t).\]
Sign conventions: \(v > 0\) means the particle is moving to the right; \(v < 0\) means it is moving to the left.

If \(m\) is the mass of the particle, Newton's second law states

\[ma(t) = F(t)\]

where \(F(t)\) is the force acting on the particle along its line of motion.

Constant Acceleration

Context: Section 1.2 treats the simple case (high-school physics level). Section 2.3 will include position-dependent forces and resistive (velocity-dependent) forces, requiring new ODE techniques from Chapter 1.

In the special case where the acceleration doesn't depend on time, \(a(t) = a\), we can integrate to obtain

\[v(t) = at + v_0\]

where \(v_0 = v(0)\) is the initial velocity. Integrating again:

\[x(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,a\,t^2 + v_0\,t + x_0,\]

where \(x_0 = x(0)\) is the initial position.

Example: Lunar Lander

Example — Rocket Science

A lunar lander is falling freely toward the surface of the moon at a speed of 450 m/s. Its thrusters cause the lander to decelerate at 2.5 m/s² (after the moon's gravitational acceleration is accounted for). At what height above the surface should the thrusters be activated to ensure a "soft touchdown" (\(v = 0\) on impact)?

Setup

Let \(x\) measure height above the moon's surface, with \(x = 0\) at the surface. Let \(t = 0\) be the moment the thrusters are activated.

  • Initial position: \(x(0) = x_0\) (unknown — this is what we want to find)
  • Initial velocity: \(v(0) = -450\) m/s (negative because the lander is falling downward)
  • Thruster acceleration: \(a = +2.5\) m/s² (positive, opposing the downward motion)

Step 1: Find \(v(t)\)

Integrate \(a(t) = 2.5\):

\[v(t) = \int 2.5\, dt = 2.5\,t + C\]

Using \(v(0) = -450\), we get \(C = -450\):

\[v(t) = 2.5\,t - 450\]

Step 2: Find \(x(t)\)

Integrate \(v(t)\):

\[x(t) = \int (2.5\,t - 450)\, dt = 1.25\,t^2 - 450\,t + C\]

Using \(x(0) = x_0\):

\[x(t) = 1.25\,t^2 - 450\,t + x_0\]

Step 3: Apply landing conditions

Let \(T\) be the landing time. We need two conditions:

  • \(v(T) = 0\) — soft landing (zero velocity at impact)
  • \(x(T) = 0\) — the lander reaches the surface

From \(v(T) = 0\):

\[0 = 2.5\,T - 450 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad T = \frac{450}{2.5} = 180 \text{ s}\]

From \(x(T) = 0\):

\[0 = 1.25(180)^2 - 450(180) + x_0\] \[x_0 = 450(180) - 1.25(180)^2 = 81000 - 40500\]
\[x_0 = 40{,}500 \text{ m}\]

The thrusters should be activated at a height of 40,500 meters above the surface.

Example: Projectile Motion

Example

A projectile is fired straight upward with an initial velocity of 100 m/s from the top of a building 20 m high and falls to the ground at the base of the building. Find:

  1. its maximum height above the ground
  2. when it passes the top of the building (on the way down)
  3. its total time in the air

Setup

Let \(x\) measure height above the ground (upward positive), with \(t = 0\) at launch.

  • Acceleration: \(a(t) = -9.8\) m/s² (gravity acts downward)
  • Initial velocity: \(v(0) = 100\) m/s
  • Initial position: \(x(0) = 20\) m (top of the building)

Finding \(v(t)\) and \(x(t)\)

Integrate the acceleration:

\[v(t) = \int (-9.8)\, dt = -9.8\,t + C\]

With \(v(0) = 100\):

\[v(t) = -9.8\,t + 100\]

Integrate the velocity:

\[x(t) = \int (-9.8\,t + 100)\, dt = -4.9\,t^2 + 100\,t + C\]

With \(x(0) = 20\):

\[x(t) = -4.9\,t^2 + 100\,t + 20\]

(a) Maximum Height

The maximum height occurs when \(v(t) = 0\) (the projectile momentarily stops before falling back down):

\[0 = -9.8\,t_1 + 100 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad t_1 = \frac{100}{9.8} \approx 10.204 \text{ s}\]

Substitute into \(x(t)\):

\[x(t_1) = -4.9\left(\frac{100}{9.8}\right)^2 + 100\left(\frac{100}{9.8}\right) + 20 \approx 530.4 \text{ m}\]

(b) When It Passes the Top of the Building

Set \(x(t) = 20\) and solve:

\[20 = -4.9\,t^2 + 100\,t + 20\] \[0 = t(-4.9\,t + 100)\]

So \(t = 0\) (launch) or:

\[t = \frac{100}{4.9} \approx 20.408 \text{ s}\]

(c) Total Time in the Air

Set \(x(t) = 0\) (ground level) and solve using the quadratic formula:

\[0 = -4.9\,t^2 + 100\,t + 20\]

Apply the quadratic formula and choose the positive root:

\[t = \frac{-100 \pm \sqrt{100^2 + 4(4.9)(20)}}{2(-4.9)} = \frac{-100 \pm \sqrt{10392}}{-9.8}\]
\[t \approx 20.604 \text{ s}\]

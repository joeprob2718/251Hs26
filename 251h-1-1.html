1. Introduction

Most interesting natural phenomena involve change, and the laws governing these phenomena are written in mathematical equations relating changing quantities.

Definition. An equation relating an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives is called a differential equation.

In this course we will study linear and nonlinear equations, progressing from first-order ODEs through higher-order ODEs, systems of ODEs, and finally PDEs. Nonlinear equations tend to be more complicated — and more interesting.

2. Examples of Differential Equations

A first-order ODE

The differential equation

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = y^2 + x^2 \]

involves the unknown function \(y(x)\) and its derivative \(y'(x) = \tfrac{dy}{dx}\). This is a first-order ODE. Because the independent variable \(x\) appears explicitly on the right-hand side, the equation is not autonomous. It has no parameters.

A second-order ODE (mass–spring–dashpot)

The differential equation

\[ m\,\frac{d^2 y}{dt^2} + b\,\frac{dy}{dt} + k\,y = 0 \]

involves the unknown function \(y(t)\) — the displacement from equilibrium of a mass attached to a spring — together with its first and second derivatives \(y'(t)\) and \(y''(t)\). This is a second-order ODE.

Here \(m\), \(b\), and \(k\) are physical parameters: \(m\) is the mass, \(b\) is the damping constant (from a shock absorber/dashpot), and \(k\) is the spring constant (measuring stiffness). The restoring force from the spring satisfies \(F = -ky\), and the resistive force from the dashpot is proportional to velocity.

When \(y > 0\) the spring is stretched; when \(y < 0\) it is compressed. This equation is autonomous (no explicit \(t\) on the right-hand side). We will study it in detail in Sections 3.4 and 3.6, including the case of a sinusoidal driving force and the phenomenon of resonance.

A second-order PDE (heat/diffusion equation)

The differential equation

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \]

involves the unknown function \(u(x,t)\) and its partial derivatives \(\tfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}\) and \(\tfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\). This is the heat (diffusion) equation for a thin, one-dimensional rod occupying \(0 \le x \le L\). It models the transfer of heat energy by conduction. Here \(k\) is the thermal diffusivity, and \(u(x,t)\) represents the temperature at position \(x\) at time \(t\).

This is a second-order PDE. We will study it in Chapter 9, along with the wave equation.

3. Key Vocabulary

Order. The order of a differential equation is the order of the highest derivative appearing in the equation. An \(n\)th-order ODE for \(y(x)\) takes the form \[ F\!\bigl(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n)}\bigr) = 0. \]
ODE vs. PDE. An ordinary differential equation (ODE) involves an unknown function of one independent variable. Most of this course (Chapters 1–7) focuses on ODEs. A partial differential equation (PDE) involves an unknown function of multiple independent variables (Chapter 9).
Autonomous. An autonomous ODE has no explicit dependence on the independent variable. The "laws of nature" (the DE) are static in "time."
Parameters. A differential equation will often contain physical constants / physical parameters in addition to the independent and dependent variables. Units are very useful in helping us formulate models!

4. Checking / Verifying vs. Solving

There is a difference between checking (verifying) whether a proposed function satisfies an equation versus solving the equation from scratch.

Algebraic analogy. To check whether \(x = 3\) is a solution of \(2x + 5 = 11\), we substitute: \[ 2(3) + 5 \stackrel{?}{=} 11 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 11 \stackrel{\checkmark}{=} 11. \quad\text{Yes!} \] To solve from scratch means to systematically "undo" the equation without guessing: \[ 2x + 5 = 11 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2x = 6 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 3. \]

5. Verifying a Solution

Example. Verify that \(y(x) = C\,e^{x^2}\) satisfies the DE \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy \] for any constant \(C\).

Compute the derivative of the proposed solution: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = C\,e^{x^2}\cdot 2x. \] Now check: does \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} \stackrel{?}{=} 2x\,y\)? \[ C\,e^{x^2}\cdot 2x \;=\; 2x \cdot C\,e^{x^2}. \quad\checkmark \] Yes — the equation holds for all \(x\) and any value of the constant \(C\). The solution makes the DE true on an interval of \(x\) (in fact, for all \(x\)).

General vs. Particular Solution. We say \(y(x) = C\,e^{x^2}\) is the general solution — it contains an integration constant \(C\) and represents all possible solutions. A specific choice such as \(y(x) = 5\,e^{x^2}\) is a particular solution.

6. Course Overview

We will spend most of this course studying ordinary differential equations and systems of ODEs, using a variety of techniques: calculus-based methods, algebraic methods, linear algebra, numerical methods, power series, and "complex frequency analysis" (the Laplace transform). We will often learn several ways to solve the same type of equation.

Towards the end of the course we will shift to partial differential equations for unknown functions of two variables, using Fourier series as our main tool.

7. Basic Goals of Applied Math

  1. Formulate: Discover the differential equation that describes a specified physical situation.
  2. Analyze: Find the appropriate solution of that equation (either exactly or approximately).
  3. Interpret: Interpret the solution in the applied context.

The cycle is: Real-world situation → Formulation → Mathematical model → Mathematical analysis → Mathematical results → Interpretation → back to the real-world situation.

Recall: "proportional to" means \(y \propto x\), which is equivalent to \(y = kx\) for some constant of proportionality \(k\).

8. Newton's Law of Cooling

A small object with variable temperature \(T(t)\) sits inside a large surrounding medium maintained at a constant ambient temperature \(A\). The rate of change of \(T(t)\) is proportional to the temperature difference between the object and the surrounding medium:

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = k(A - T), \qquad k > 0. \]
Sanity check. When \(A > T\) (cold object in warm surroundings): \(A - T > 0\), so \(\frac{dT}{dt} > 0\) — the object heats up. ✓
When \(A < T\) (hot object in cool surroundings): \(A - T < 0\), so \(\frac{dT}{dt} < 0\) — the object cools down. ✓

One could alternatively write \(\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - A)\) with \(k < 0\), but the convention above (with \(k > 0\)) is standard.

This "first-pass" model is an autonomous ODE (no explicit \(t\)). If the surroundings had a known, time-varying temperature \(A(t)\), we would instead write \[ \frac{dT}{dt} = k\bigl(A(t) - T\bigr), \] which is a non-autonomous equation and more complicated to analyze.

9. Torricelli's Law

The volume rate \(\frac{dV}{dt}\) at which water leaks from a tank is proportional to the square root of the depth \(y(t)\) of water in the tank:

\[ \frac{dV}{dt} = -k\sqrt{y}, \qquad k > 0. \]

This single equation has two unknown functions, \(V(t)\) and \(y(t)\), so it is not a closed equation by itself. To close it, we need a relationship between \(V\) and \(y\).

If the tank has vertical sides and constant cross-sectional area \(A\), then \(V = Ay\) and \(\frac{dV}{dt} = A\,\frac{dy}{dt}\), giving

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} = -h\sqrt{y}, \qquad h = \frac{k}{A} > 0. \]

For more complicated tank shapes, we relate \(y(t)\) and \(V(t)\) using a volume integral: \(V = \int_0^y A(z)\,dz\), where \(A(z)\) is the cross-sectional area at height \(z\).

10. Simple Population Growth / Decay

The time rate of change of \(P(t)\), the population at time \(t\), is proportional to the population:

\[ \frac{dP}{dt} = kP \]

for some constant \(k\). The general solution is

\[ P(t) = C\,e^{kt}. \]

To verify: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = C\,e^{kt}\cdot k\), and \(kP = k\cdot C\,e^{kt}\). These are equal. ✓

Example. Suppose a population has initial value \(P(0) = 1000\) and doubles in one hour. Find the particular solution.

Step 1 — Find \(C\):
\(P(0) = C\,e^{k\cdot 0} = C = 1000\), so \(P(t) = 1000\,e^{kt}\).

Step 2 — Find \(k\): (with \(t\) in hours)
\(P(1) = 2 \cdot P(0)\) gives \(1000\,e^k = 2000\), so \(e^k = 2\) and \(k = \ln 2\).

Particular solution: \[ P(t) = 1000\,e^{t\ln 2} = 1000 \cdot 2^t. \]

Limitation. Notice that \(\lim_{t\to\infty} P(t) = \infty\) — this model predicts unbounded growth. In reality, populations run out of space and food. A more refined model is needed (see the logistic model below).

General solution as a one-parameter family

For the first-order ODE \(\frac{dP}{dt} = (\ln 2)\,P\), the general solution \(P(t) = C\,e^{t\ln 2}\) is a one-parameter family of functions indexed by \(C\).

Initial Value Problem (IVP). A DE "typically" has infinitely many solutions; an initial value problem "typically" has one particular solution. An initial value problem consists of a DE together with initial condition(s).

For example:

\[ \begin{cases} \dfrac{dP}{dt} = (\ln 2)\,P & \text{(DE)}\\[6pt] P(0) = 1000 & \text{(IC)} \end{cases} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad P(t) = 1000\,e^{t\ln 2} \;\text{(unique solution)}. \]

We will discuss conditions for existence and uniqueness of IVP solutions in Section 1.3.

11. Different "Rates of Change"

Instead of the "standard" time rate of change \(P'(t)\) (which has units of, say, people per year), we can consider the relative rate of change, normalized by population:

\[ \frac{P'(t)}{P(t)} = \frac{1}{P}\,\frac{dP}{dt}. \]

This is like a "percent rate of change" (without the factor of 100). In the simple population model, the relative rate of change is constant: \[ \frac{1}{P}\,\frac{dP}{dt} = k \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{dP}{dt} = kP. \]

12. Logistic Population Model

Let \(M > 0\) represent the carrying capacity — the maximum population the habitat can sustain. The relative rate of change of \(P(t)\) is proportional to the "available space" \((M - P)\):

\[ \frac{1}{P}\,\frac{dP}{dt} = k(M - P), \qquad\text{so}\qquad \frac{dP}{dt} = kP(M - P), \qquad k > 0. \]

Unlike the simple exponential model, the logistic model predicts that solutions approach the carrying capacity \(P = M\) as \(t \to \infty\).

We will explore this model further in Sections 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, and 2.2.

13. Second-Order Example

Example. Show that the two-parameter family \[ y(x) = A\cos(3x) + B\sin(3x) \] solves the ODE \(\;y'' + 9y = 0\).

Compute successive derivatives:

\[ y = A\cos 3x + B\sin 3x, \] \[ y' = -3A\sin 3x + 3B\cos 3x, \] \[ y'' = -9A\cos 3x - 9B\sin 3x. \]

Substitute into the DE:

\[ y'' + 9y = \bigl(-9A\cos 3x - 9B\sin 3x\bigr) + 9\bigl(A\cos 3x + B\sin 3x\bigr) = 0. \quad\checkmark \]

This holds for all \(x\) and any constants \(A\) and \(B\). Since the ODE is second-order, the general solution has two free parameters.

14. Normal Form and General Solutions

Normal form. The normal form of an \(n\)th-order ODE is \[ y^{(n)} = G\!\bigl(x,\, y,\, y',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n-1)}\bigr). \]

Typically, the general solution of an \(n\)th-order ODE will be an \(n\)-parameter family of solutions, indexed by \(n\) "integration constants" — one for each of the \(n\) derivatives that must be "undone."

Notation: \(y',\; y'',\; y'''\) for the first three derivatives; then \(y^{(4)}\) for the fourth derivative (not to be confused with \(y^4\), the fourth power).

Section 2.3: Acceleration and Velocity Models

Introduction

In Section 1.2, we solved basic position–velocity–acceleration problems with constant acceleration. Newton's second law reduced to

\[ m\frac{dv}{dt} = \text{constant}, \]

which could be immediately integrated (it's "not really a differential equation"). Integrating once gives \(v(t)\), and integrating again gives the position \(x(t)\).

Now that we know how to solve separable and linear first-order equations, we can consider more interesting scenarios:

  • Constant gravity + speed-dependent air resistance: The resistive force has the form \(F_R = k|v|^p\) where \(1 \le p \le 2\) is physically relevant, depending on the object's shape and the speed regime.
  • Variable gravitational acceleration: Using the "real" inverse-square law for gravity, rather than the sea-level approximation \(F = mg\) with \(g = 9.8\;\text{m/s}^2\).

Example 1 — Constant Gravity, No Air Resistance

Problem A crossbow bolt is fired straight upward from the ground (\(y_0 = 0\)) with initial velocity \(v_0 = 49\;\text{m/s}\). Gravitational acceleration is \(g = 9.8\;\text{m/s}^2\). Find the bolt's height function \(y(t)\).

Solution

Taking upward as positive, the only force is gravity (downward), so by Newton's second law (\(ma = F\)):

\[ m\frac{dv}{dt} = -9.8\,m \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dv}{dt} = -9.8. \]

Integrate directly:

\[ v(t) = -9.8t + C. \]

Applying \(v(0) = 49\) gives \(C = 49\), so

\[ v(t) = -9.8t + 49. \]

Integrate again to find position:

\[ y(t) = \int v(t)\,dt = -4.9t^2 + 49t + C. \]

Applying \(y(0) = 0\) gives \(C = 0\), so

\[ \boxed{y(t) = -4.9t^2 + 49t.} \]

Example 2 — Gravity with Linear Air Resistance

Problem Consider the same bolt (\(y(0)=0\), \(v(0)=49\;\text{m/s}\)). Now the air resistance force is proportional to velocity, with proportionality constant \(0.04m\) (where \(m\) is the mass). Find the bolt's height function.

Setting Up the ODE

By Newton's second law:

\[ m\frac{dv}{dt} = -9.8m - 0.04m\,v. \]
Understanding the forces
  • When the arrow goes up (\(v > 0\)): Both gravity and air resistance point downward — they cooperate to reduce the upward velocity.
  • When the arrow comes down (\(v < 0\)): Gravity points down but air resistance points up — they oppose each other.
In both cases, the equation \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = -9.8 - 0.04v\) correctly handles the signs.

Dividing by \(m\), we obtain the IVP:

\[ \frac{dv}{dt} = -9.8 - 0.04v, \qquad v(0) = 49. \]

Solving via Integrating Factor

This is both separable and linear. Using the linear method, rewrite in standard form:

\[ \frac{dv}{dt} + 0.04\,v = -9.8. \]

Integrating factor: \(\mu(t) = e^{\int 0.04\,dt} = e^{0.04t}\).

Multiply both sides:

\[ e^{0.04t}\frac{dv}{dt} + 0.04\,e^{0.04t}\,v = -9.8\,e^{0.04t}. \]

Recognize product rule:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(e^{0.04t}\,v\right) = -9.8\,e^{0.04t}. \]

Integrate: (using \(\frac{1}{0.04} = 25\))

\[ e^{0.04t}\,v = \int(-9.8\,e^{0.04t})\,dt = -9.8 \cdot 25\,e^{0.04t} + C = -245\,e^{0.04t} + C. \] \[ v = -245 + Ce^{-0.04t}. \]

Apply \(v(0) = 49\):

\[ 49 = -245 + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C = 294. \]
\[ \boxed{v(t) = -245 + 294\,e^{-0.04t}.} \]

Finding the Position

Integrate \(v(t)\) to get \(y(t)\):

\[ y(t) = \int v(t)\,dt = -245t - \frac{294}{0.04}\,e^{-0.04t} + C = -245t - 25(294)\,e^{-0.04t} + C. \]

Apply \(y(0) = 0\):

\[ 0 = -25(294) + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C = 25(294). \]
\[ \boxed{y(t) = -245t - 25(294)\,e^{-0.04t} + 25(294).} \]

Example 3 — Motorboat with Quadratic Water Resistance

Problem A motorboat starts from rest (\(v(0) = 0\)). Its motor provides a constant acceleration of \(4\;\text{ft/s}^2\), but water resistance causes a deceleration of \(\dfrac{v^2}{400}\;\text{ft/s}^2\). Find the limiting velocity \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} v(t)\).

Solution

The net acceleration equation is (phrased in terms of acceleration, not force):

\[ \frac{dv}{dt} = 4 - \frac{v^2}{400}, \qquad v(0) = 0, \quad v \ge 0. \]

This is separable, but solving explicitly leads to messy partial fractions. Instead, we use a phase line analysis to predict the long-term behavior.

Find equilibria: Set \(4 - \dfrac{v^2}{400} = 0\):

\[ v^2 = 1600 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad v = \pm 40. \]

Since \(v \ge 0\), the relevant equilibrium is \(v = 40\).

Sign analysis:

  • For \(0 \le v < 40\): \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} > 0\) (velocity increasing).
  • For \(v > 40\): \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} < 0\) (velocity decreasing).

Therefore \(v = 40\) is a stable equilibrium. Since the boat starts at \(v(0) = 0\), which is below the equilibrium, its velocity increases toward 40.

\[ \boxed{\lim_{t \to \infty} v(t) = 40 \;\text{ft/s}.} \]
Key insight We didn't need to find the explicit formula for \(v(t)\) — the phase line analysis from Section 2.2 gave us the limiting behavior directly!

Escape Velocity

The Inverse-Square Law

Instead of the sea-level approximation \(F = mg\), the "real" Newtonian gravitational force is

\[ F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}, \]

where \(m\) and \(M\) are the masses of the two objects, \(r\) is the distance between their centers of mass, and \(G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\;\text{N}\!\cdot\!\text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2\) is the universal gravitational constant.

To see how the constant \(g = 9.8\;\text{m/s}^2\) arises, substitute \(r = R_E = 6.371 \times 10^6\;\text{m}\) and \(M = M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\;\text{kg}\):

\[ F = \frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \cdot 5.97 \times 10^{24}}{(6.378 \times 10^6)^2}\,m \approx 9.8\,m. \]

This approximation works near Earth's surface, but for problems involving rocket escape velocity we need the full inverse-square law.

Setting Up the ODE

Consider a rocket launched from Earth's surface. After the booster engines are detached, the only force is gravitational attraction:

\[ m\frac{dv}{dt} = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}. \]

Dividing by \(m\):

\[ \frac{dv}{dt} = -\frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

Since \(v = \dfrac{dr}{dt}\), this is really \(\dfrac{d^2r}{dt^2} = -\dfrac{GM}{r^2}\), a second-order nonlinear ODE for \(r(t)\). We don't have standard methods for this type of equation.

The Chain Rule Trick

Suppose \(v(t) > 0\), so \(r(t)\) is increasing and invertible. We can write velocity as a function of position: \(v = v(r)\). By the chain rule:

Chain rule trick \[ \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{dv}{dr}\cdot\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{dv}{dr}\cdot v = v\frac{dv}{dr}. \]

This converts our second-order ODE for \(r(t)\) into a first-order separable equation for \(v(r)\):

\[ v\frac{dv}{dr} = -\frac{GM}{r^2}. \]
Note This trick — converting acceleration from a function of time to a function of position — is also used in EMech 212 and Phys 419.

Solving by Separation

Separate and integrate:

\[ \int v\,dv = \int\!\left(-\frac{GM}{r^2}\right)dr. \] \[ \frac{v^2}{2} = \frac{GM}{r} + C. \]
Energy conservation Multiplying through by \(m\) and rearranging gives \[ \frac{mv^2}{2} - \frac{GMm}{r} = C_1, \] which says kinetic energy + gravitational potential energy = constant. We have just derived conservation of energy from Newton's second law!

Applying the Initial Condition

Use \(v = v_0\) when \(r = R\) (launched from Earth's surface):

\[ C = \frac{v_0^2}{2} - \frac{GM}{R}. \]

Substituting back:

\[ \frac{v^2}{2} = \frac{GM}{r} + \frac{v_0^2}{2} - \frac{GM}{R}, \]

or equivalently,

\[ v^2 = v_0^2 - \frac{2GM}{R} + \frac{2GM}{r}. \]

Note that \(\dfrac{2GM}{r} \to 0\) as \(r \to \infty\).

Two Cases

Case 1: If \(v_0^2 - \dfrac{2GM}{R} < 0\), there exists some \(r = r_1\) at which \(v^2 = 0\), meaning \(v = 0\). The rocket stops and is pulled back to Earth.

Case 2: If \(v_0^2 - \dfrac{2GM}{R} \ge 0\), then \(v^2 > 0\) for all \(r \ge R\). Since \(v(r)\) is continuous and \(v_0 = v(R) > 0\), the velocity can never pass through zero, so \(v > 0\) for all \(r \ge R\). The rocket never stops moving away from Earth.

The Escape Velocity Formula

The critical threshold is \(v_0^2 - \dfrac{2GM}{R} = 0\), giving:

\[ \boxed{v_{\text{escape}} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}\,.} \]

This is the minimum initial velocity needed so that \(v > 0\) for all \(r \ge R\) (equivalently, for all \(t \ge 0\)). Provided \(v_0 > \sqrt{2GM/R}\), one can show (see textbook problem 2.3.27) that \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} r(t) = \infty\), so the rocket truly "escapes" Earth.

Subtle point Just because \(v(t) > 0\) for all \(t\) does not automatically guarantee \(\lim_{t\to\infty} r(t) = \infty\) — it is possible for \(r(t)\) to increase but approach a finite limit. However, for this problem it can be verified that \(r(t) \to \infty\) when \(v_0 \ge \sqrt{2GM/R}\).

Velocity-Dependent vs. Position-Dependent Forces

When the force depends on velocity (like air or water resistance), Newton's second law gives a first-order ODE of the form

\[ \frac{dv}{dt} = f(v), \]

which we solve using standard first-order techniques (separable or linear).

These velocity-dependent forces are non-conservative — they do not arise from a potential energy. Resistive forces convert kinetic/potential energy into heat. The total energy of the universe is conserved, but the mechanical energy (kinetic + potential) is not constant in time.

When the force depends on position (like gravity via the inverse-square law), we get a second-order ODE. The chain rule trick \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = v\dfrac{dv}{dr}\) converts it into a first-order separable equation for \(v(r)\), and the resulting conservation law is energy conservation.

Example 4 — Coasting Boat with Linear Drag

Problem A motorboat coasts (no motor acceleration) and experiences water resistance proportional to its speed: \[ \frac{dv}{dt} = -kv, \qquad k > 0. \] Find \(v(t)\) and \(x(t)\) in terms of the initial position \(x_0\) and initial velocity \(v_0 > 0\). Does the boat travel a finite distance, or does it coast infinitely far?

Solution

This is the standard exponential decay equation \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = -kv\), with solution

\[ \boxed{v(t) = v_0\,e^{-kt}.} \]

Integrate to find position:

\[ x(t) = \int v_0\,e^{-kt}\,dt = -\frac{v_0}{k}\,e^{-kt} + C. \]

Apply \(x(0) = x_0\):

\[ x_0 = -\frac{v_0}{k} + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C = x_0 + \frac{v_0}{k}. \]
\[ \boxed{x(t) = -\frac{v_0}{k}\,e^{-kt} + x_0 + \frac{v_0}{k}.} \]

Does the boat travel a finite distance?

As \(t \to \infty\), \(e^{-kt} \to 0\), so

\[ \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = x_0 + \frac{v_0}{k}. \]

Yes — the boat travels a finite total distance of \(\dfrac{v_0}{k}\) from its starting point, even though \(v(t) > 0\) for all \(t \ge 0\) (the velocity never reaches zero in finite time).

Note on uniqueness The fact that \(v(t) = v_0 e^{-kt} > 0\) for all finite \(t\) means the boat never actually stops in finite time. There is no "stopping time" \(T\) at which \(v(T) = 0\). For air/water resistance proportional to \(|v|^p\) with \(1 \le p \le 2\), this is always the case — if a solution reached \(v = 0\) in finite time and then stayed at \(v = 0\), it would cross the equilibrium solution \(v \equiv 0\), violating uniqueness of solutions (since \(f(v) = -kv^p\) satisfies the conditions for unique solutions in this range).

Example 5 — Coasting Boat with Quadratic Drag

Problem A motorboat coasts and experiences water resistance proportional to its speed squared: \[ \frac{dv}{dt} = -kv^2, \qquad k > 0. \] Find \(v(t)\) and \(x(t)\) in terms of \(x_0\) and \(v_0 > 0\). Does the boat travel a finite distance?

Solution

This is separable. Separate variables:

\[ \frac{dv}{v^2} = -k\,dt \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int v^{-2}\,dv = \int -k\,dt. \] \[ -\frac{1}{v} = -kt + C_1 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{1}{v} = kt + C_1. \]

Apply \(v(0) = v_0\): \(\dfrac{1}{v_0} = C_1\). So

\[ \frac{1}{v} = kt + \frac{1}{v_0} = \frac{kv_0 t + 1}{v_0}. \]
\[ \boxed{v(t) = \frac{v_0}{1 + kv_0 t}.} \]

Integrate to find position:

\[ x(t) = \int \frac{v_0}{1 + kv_0 t}\,dt = \frac{1}{k}\ln(1 + kv_0 t) + C. \]

Apply \(x(0) = x_0\): \(C = x_0\).

\[ \boxed{x(t) = x_0 + \frac{1}{k}\ln(1 + kv_0 t).} \]

Does the boat travel a finite distance?

As \(t \to \infty\):

\[ \lim_{t\to\infty} x(t) = x_0 + \frac{1}{k}\lim_{t\to\infty}\ln(1 + kv_0 t) = \infty. \]

No — the boat coasts infinitely far! Even though the velocity \(v(t) \to 0\) as \(t \to \infty\), the boat slows down so gradually that the total distance traveled is unbounded.

Comparison
  • Linear drag (\(F_R \propto v\)): velocity decays exponentially → boat travels a finite total distance.
  • Quadratic drag (\(F_R \propto v^2\)): velocity decays like \(1/t\) → boat travels an infinite total distance.
The quadratic-drag velocity decays more slowly, allowing the integral of \(v(t)\) (i.e., total displacement) to diverge.

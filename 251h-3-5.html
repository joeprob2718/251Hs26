1. Overview & Vocabulary

We have been solving constant-coefficient homogeneous differential equations of the form

\[ a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = 0. \]

The method of undetermined coefficients is a technique to solve inhomogeneous equations of the form

\[ a_n y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1 y' + a_0 y = f(x), \]

where \(f(x)\) (the source term, inhomogeneity, or external force) is a linear combination of the types of functions that appear in general solutions of constant-coefficient ODEs: exponentials, sines, cosines, polynomials, and products thereof.

Applicability

Undetermined coefficients works when \(f(x)\) is built from \(e^{ax}\), \(\cos(bx)\), \(\sin(bx)\), polynomials in \(x\), and products of these. It does not apply to source terms like \(\tan x\), \(\sqrt{x}\), \(\ln x\), etc., because those functions keep producing new independent terms under differentiation.

2. The Three-Step Process

  1. Find the complementary solution \(y_c\). Solve the associated homogeneous equation \(P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right) y = 0\) using previously discussed techniques (characteristic polynomial, etc.). The result \(y_c\) is the general solution of the homogeneous problem — it contains all the free constants \(c_1, c_2, \ldots\)
  2. Find a particular solution \(y_p\). Formulate an "undetermined coefficients ansatz" for \(y_p\) based on the form of \(f(x)\). Substitute this ansatz into the inhomogeneous DE, match coefficients on linearly independent terms, and solve the resulting linear system to pin down the undetermined coefficients. The result \(y_p\) is one specific solution of the inhomogeneous equation — it has no free constants.
  3. Combine: \(y = y_c + y_p\). This is the general solution of the inhomogeneous problem. Apply initial conditions only at this stage to determine the constants in \(y_c\).
Important

Apply initial conditions only after forming \(y = y_c + y_p\). We need the full solution \(y = y_c + y_p\) to satisfy the initial data — not \(y_c\) alone.

3. Why \(y = y_c + y_p\) Works

Since \(y_c\) solves the homogeneous equation and \(y_p\) solves the inhomogeneous equation, the linearity (superposition) of the differential operator gives:

\[ P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right)(y_c + y_p) = P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right) y_c + P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right) y_p = 0 + f(x) = f(x). \]

So \(y = y_c + y_p\) indeed solves the inhomogeneous problem. This is analogous to the linear algebra fact that if \(A\vec{x} = \vec{b}\) has a particular solution \(\vec{x}_p\), then every solution is of the form \(\vec{x}_p + \vec{x}_c\) where \(\vec{x}_c\) is in the null space of \(A\).

4. How to Build the \(y_p\) Ansatz

The source \(f(x)\) must be a linear combination of functions of the form

\[ e^{ax},\quad e^{ax}\cos(bx),\quad e^{ax}\sin(bx), \]

(where \(a = 0\) is allowed, covering pure polynomials and trig), as well as polynomial multiples:

\[ x^n e^{ax},\quad x^n e^{ax}\cos(bx),\quad x^n e^{ax}\sin(bx), \qquad n \text{ a positive integer}. \]

These are precisely the functions for which the process of repeated differentiation eventually stops producing new linearly independent terms.

Building the initial guess

For each term in \(f(x)\), take derivatives indefinitely and collect all distinct linearly independent functions that appear. The initial guess for \(y_p\) is an arbitrary linear combination of those functions, with undetermined coefficients \(A, B, C, \ldots\)

The matchup adjustment

If any term in the initial guess also appears in \(y_c\), substitution into the DE will cause those terms to cancel and we'll have "nothing to work with" — leading to an inconsistent system. To fix this, multiply the offending group of terms by the minimum power of \(x\) needed so that no term in \(y_p\) coincides with any term in \(y_c\) (or with other groups in \(y_p\)).

Why find \(y_c\) first?

You must always compute \(y_c\) before constructing \(y_p\), because you need to check for matchups. The two steps cannot be done in arbitrary order.

5. Solving for the Coefficients

Once you have the final \(y_p\) ansatz (after any matchup adjustments), substitute it into the inhomogeneous DE. Both sides will be linear combinations of the same linearly independent functions. Matching corresponding coefficients (just like in partial fractions) yields a linear system of equations. Solve this system by elimination, substitution, or row reduction to find the undetermined coefficients.

6. Example: No Matchup (Trig Source)

Example

Solve the initial value problem

\[ y'' + 5y' + 6y = 156\cos(2x), \qquad y(0) = 2,\quad y'(0) = 4. \]

Step 1: Find \(y_c\)

Characteristic equation: \(r^2 + 5r + 6 = (r+2)(r+3) = 0\), so \(r = -2, -3\).

\[ y_c = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 e^{-3x}. \]

Step 2: Find \(y_p\)

The source is \(f(x) = 156\cos(2x)\). Taking derivatives: \(f'(x) = -312\sin(2x)\), \(f''(x) = -624\cos(2x)\), and then nothing new appears. The linearly independent terms are \(\cos(2x)\) and \(\sin(2x)\).

Initial guess: \(y_p = A\cos(2x) + B\sin(2x)\).

Checking against \(y_c\): no terms in common, so no adjustment needed.

Compute derivatives and substitute:

\[ y_p' = -2A\sin(2x) + 2B\cos(2x), \qquad y_p'' = -4A\cos(2x) - 4B\sin(2x). \]

Substituting into \(y'' + 5y' + 6y = 156\cos(2x)\):

\[ (-4A + 10B + 6A)\cos(2x) + (-4B - 10A + 6B)\sin(2x) = 156\cos(2x). \]
\[ (2A + 10B)\cos(2x) + (-10A + 2B)\sin(2x) = 156\cos(2x) + 0\cdot\sin(2x). \]

Matching coefficients on the linearly independent functions \(\cos(2x)\) and \(\sin(2x)\):

\[ \begin{cases} 2A + 10B = 156 \\ -10A + 2B = 0 \end{cases} \]

From the second equation: \(B = 5A\). Substituting: \(2A + 50A = 156\), so \(A = 3\) and \(B = 15\).

\[ \boxed{y_p = 3\cos(2x) + 15\sin(2x).} \]

Step 3: General solution and initial conditions

\[ y = y_c + y_p = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 e^{-3x} + 3\cos(2x) + 15\sin(2x). \]
\[ y' = -2c_1 e^{-2x} - 3c_2 e^{-3x} - 6\sin(2x) + 30\cos(2x). \]

Applying \(y(0) = 2\) and \(y'(0) = 4\):

\[ \begin{cases} c_1 + c_2 + 3 = 2 \\ -2c_1 - 3c_2 + 30 = 4 \end{cases} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \begin{cases} c_1 + c_2 = -1 \\ -2c_1 - 3c_2 = -26 \end{cases} \]

Solving: \(c_1 = -29\), \(c_2 = 28\). (You can verify by elimination.) The final answer is

\[ y = -29e^{-2x} + 28e^{-3x} + 3\cos(2x) + 15\sin(2x). \]

7. Incorrect Ansatz Leads to an Inconsistent System

If the ansatz is missing necessary terms, or if it duplicates terms from \(y_c\), the coefficient-matching step will produce a system of equations with no solution. Here are illustrations of each mistake.

Example — Missing linearly independent terms

Trying \(y_p = A\cos(2x)\) alone (without a \(\sin(2x)\) term) for \(y'' + 5y' + 6y = 3\cos(2x)\) leads to:

\[ 2A\cos(2x) - 10A\sin(2x) = 3\cos(2x) + 0\cdot\sin(2x), \]

giving \(2A = 3\) and \(-10A = 0\) — which is inconsistent. The fix: include both \(A\cos(2x)\) and \(B\sin(2x)\).

Example — Matchup with \(y_c\) (not adjusted)

For \(y'' + 4y = 3\cos(2x)\), the complementary solution is \(y_c = c_1\cos(2x) + c_2\sin(2x)\). Trying \(y_p = A\cos(2x) + B\sin(2x)\) gives:

\[ (-4A\cos 2x - 4B\sin 2x) + 4(A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x) = 3\cos(2x), \]
\[ 0 = 3\cos(2x) \quad \text{for all } x \quad — \text{impossible!} \]

The \(y_p\) terms cancel perfectly because they solve the homogeneous equation. The fix: multiply by \(x\) to get the adjusted ansatz \(y_p = Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x)\).

Analogy with partial fractions

This is exactly analogous to using the wrong form in a partial fraction decomposition. For instance, trying \(\frac{1}{x^3+x} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{B}{x^2+1}\) (missing the linear numerator \(Bx + C\) over the irreducible quadratic) leads to an inconsistent system \(A = 0,\; B = 0,\; A = 1\).

8. Example: Matchup Case (Resonance)

Example

Solve the initial value problem

\[ y'' + 4y = 3\cos(2x), \qquad y(0) = 2,\quad y'(0) = 4. \]

Step 1: Find \(y_c\)

Characteristic equation: \(r^2 + 4 = 0\), so \(r = \pm 2i\).

\[ y_c = c_1\cos(2x) + c_2\sin(2x). \]

Step 2: Find \(y_p\)

Source: \(f(x) = 3\cos(2x)\). The linearly independent terms from its derivatives are \(\cos(2x)\) and \(\sin(2x)\).

Initial guess: \(y_p = A\cos(2x) + B\sin(2x)\).

Matchup! Both \(\cos(2x)\) and \(\sin(2x)\) appear in \(y_c\). Multiply by \(x\):

Adjusted guess: \(y_p = Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x)\).

Computing derivatives (product rule):

\[ y_p' = A\cos(2x) + B\sin(2x) - 2Ax\sin(2x) + 2Bx\cos(2x). \]
\[ y_p'' = -4A\sin(2x) + 4B\cos(2x) - 4Ax\cos(2x) - 4Bx\sin(2x). \]

Substituting into \(y'' + 4y = 3\cos(2x)\):

\[ \bigl(-4A\sin(2x) + 4B\cos(2x) \underbrace{- 4Ax\cos(2x) - 4Bx\sin(2x)}_{\text{cancel with } 4y_p}\bigr) + 4\bigl(Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x)\bigr) = 3\cos(2x). \]

As expected, the \(x\cos(2x)\) and \(x\sin(2x)\) terms cancel. What remains:

\[ -4A\sin(2x) + 4B\cos(2x) = 0\cdot\sin(2x) + 3\cos(2x). \]

Matching coefficients:

\[ -4A = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; A = 0, \qquad 4B = 3 \;\Longrightarrow\; B = \tfrac{3}{4}. \]
\[ \boxed{y_p = \frac{3}{4}\,x\sin(2x).} \]

Step 3: General solution

\[ y = y_c + y_p = c_1\cos(2x) + c_2\sin(2x) + \frac{3}{4}\,x\sin(2x). \]

Apply \(y(0) = 2\) and \(y'(0) = 4\) to find \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).

Resonance

Notice that the particular solution \(y_p = \frac{3}{4}x\sin(2x)\) grows without bound as \(x \to \infty\). This is the phenomenon of resonance: when the source frequency matches the system's natural frequency, the response amplitude grows linearly in time (in the undamped case).

9. Example: Form of \(y_p\) — Exponential Source

Example

Determine the form of \(y_p\) for

\[ y'' - 2y' + 2y = xe^{2x}. \]

Step 1: \(y_c\)

Characteristic equation: \(r^2 - 2r + 2 = 0\), i.e., \((r-1)^2 + 1 = 0\), giving \(r = 1 \pm i\).

\[ y_c = c_1 e^x\cos x + c_2 e^x\sin x. \]

Step 2: Initial guess for \(y_p\)

Source: \(f(x) = xe^{2x}\). Taking derivatives: \(f'(x) = e^{2x} + 2xe^{2x}\), \(f''(x) = 4e^{2x} + 4xe^{2x}\), and nothing new appears beyond this. The linearly independent terms are \(xe^{2x}\) and \(e^{2x}\).

Initial guess: \(y_p = Axe^{2x} + Be^{2x}\).

Checking against \(y_c\): the complementary solution involves \(e^x\cos x\) and \(e^x\sin x\), which are entirely different. No matchup, so no adjustment is needed.

\[ \boxed{y_p = Axe^{2x} + Be^{2x}.} \]

10. Example: Form of \(y_p\) — Matchup with \(y_c\)

Example

Determine the form of \(y_p\) for

\[ y'' - 2y' + 2y = e^x\sin x. \]

Step 1: \(y_c\)

Same as above: \(r = 1 \pm i\), so

\[ y_c = c_1 e^x\cos x + c_2 e^x\sin x. \]

Step 2: Initial guess for \(y_p\)

Source: \(f(x) = e^x\sin x\). Taking derivatives: \(f'(x) = e^x\cos x + e^x\sin x\), and then nothing new. The linearly independent terms are \(e^x\sin x\) and \(e^x\cos x\).

Initial guess: \(y_p = Ae^x\cos x + Be^x\sin x\).

Matchup! Both terms appear in \(y_c\). Multiply by \(x\):

\[ \boxed{y_p = Axe^x\cos x + Bxe^x\sin x.} \]

11. Example: Form of \(y_p\) — Multiple Source Terms

Example

Determine the form of \(y_p\) for

\[ y'' + 4y = 3x\cos(2x) + e^{3x}. \]

Step 1: \(y_c\)

\(r^2 + 4 = 0\), so \(r = \pm 2i\).

\[ y_c = c_1\cos(2x) + c_2\sin(2x). \]

Step 2: Initial guess for \(y_p\)

Source: \(f(x) = 3x\cos(2x) + e^{3x}\). Treat each part separately.

From \(3x\cos(2x)\): Differentiating repeatedly yields the linearly independent terms \(x\cos(2x)\), \(x\sin(2x)\), \(\cos(2x)\), and \(\sin(2x)\). Initial guess contribution: \(Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x) + C\cos(2x) + D\sin(2x)\).

From \(e^{3x}\): Differentiating gives only \(e^{3x}\). Initial guess contribution: \(Ee^{3x}\).

Combined initial guess:

\[ y_p = Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x) + C\cos(2x) + D\sin(2x) + Ee^{3x}. \]

Matchup check: The terms \(C\cos(2x)\) and \(D\sin(2x)\) match \(y_c\). To fix, we need to multiply the entire group of trig terms that share the same frequency by \(x\). But \(Ax\cos(2x)\) and \(Bx\sin(2x)\) are already present, so we must go up to \(x^2\) for the matching pair. The \(e^{3x}\) term has no matchup.

Adjusted guess:

\[ \boxed{y_p = Ax\cos(2x) + Bx\sin(2x) + Cx^2\cos(2x) + Dx^2\sin(2x) + Ee^{3x}.} \]

12. Example: Fourth-Order Equation

Example

Determine the form of \(y_p\) for

\[ y^{(4)} + 9y'' = (x^2 + 1)\sin(3x) + xe^{2x}. \]

Step 1: \(y_c\)

Characteristic equation: \(r^4 + 9r^2 = r^2(r^2 + 9) = 0\), giving \(r = 0\) (double) and \(r = \pm 3i\).

\[ y_c = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3\cos(3x) + c_4\sin(3x). \]

Step 2: Initial guess for \(y_p\)

From \((x^2+1)\sin(3x)\): The linearly independent derivative terms are \(x^2\sin(3x)\), \(x^2\cos(3x)\), \(x\sin(3x)\), \(x\cos(3x)\), \(\sin(3x)\), and \(\cos(3x)\). Initial guess contribution:

\[ (Ax^2 + Bx + C)\sin(3x) + (Dx^2 + Ex + F)\cos(3x). \]

From \(xe^{2x}\): Terms are \(xe^{2x}\) and \(e^{2x}\). Contribution: \((Gx + H)e^{2x}\).

Matchup check: The terms \(\sin(3x)\) and \(\cos(3x)\) (the \(C\) and \(F\) pieces) appear in \(y_c\). Multiply the entire trig group by \(x\) — but that would create \(x\sin(3x)\) and \(x\cos(3x)\), which are already present in the guess. So multiply by \(x\) once more? Actually, we multiply the whole group uniformly: the polynomial coefficients \(Ax^2 + Bx + C\) all gain an extra factor of \(x\), becoming \(Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx\). The \(e^{2x}\) terms have no matchup.

Adjusted guess:

\[ \boxed{y_p = (Ax^2 + Bx + Cx^3)\sin(3x) + (Dx^2 + Ex + Fx^3)\cos(3x) + (Gx + H)e^{2x}.} \]

(Equivalently, rewriting with the powers in order: \(y_p = (Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx)\sin(3x) + (Dx^3 + Ex^2 + Fx)\cos(3x) + (Gx + H)e^{2x}\), where the letters have been relabeled.)

13. Shortcut Rules for the Initial Guess

Here are quick rules for forming the initial \(y_p\) guess from the source term \(f(x)\), before checking for matchups with \(y_c\):

  • Term with \(e^{ax}\): Include \(e^{ax}\) — no other new terms needed from this factor alone.
  • Term with \(\cos(bx)\) or \(\sin(bx)\): Always include both \(\cos(bx)\) and \(\sin(bx)\).
  • Term with \(x^n\): Include all lower powers \(x^{n-1}, x^{n-2}, \ldots, x, 1\) as well.
  • Products: Combine the above rules. For example, \(x^2 e^{3x}\cos(5x)\) produces an initial guess of the form \((Ax^2 + Bx + C)e^{3x}\cos(5x) + (Dx^2 + Ex + F)e^{3x}\sin(5x)\).

After forming the initial guess, check for matchups with \(y_c\) and multiply the offending group by the minimum power of \(x\) to eliminate all overlaps.

Why the extra \(x\) works (annihilator viewpoint)

The matchup adjustment can be understood via annihilators. The idea is to convert the inhomogeneous equation into a higher-order homogeneous equation by applying a differential operator that "annihilates" the source. For example, to solve \(y'' + 4y = 3\cos(2x)\), apply \(\left(\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + 4\right)\) to both sides to get the fourth-order homogeneous equation \(y^{(4)} + 8y'' + 16y = 0\), whose characteristic equation \((r^2+4)^2 = 0\) has roots \(r = \pm 2i\) (each double). The general solution of this fourth-order equation is \(c_1\cos(2x) + c_2\sin(2x) + c_3 x\cos(2x) + c_4 x\sin(2x)\). The first two terms are \(y_c\); the "new" terms \(x\cos(2x)\) and \(x\sin(2x)\) form the \(y_p\) ansatz.

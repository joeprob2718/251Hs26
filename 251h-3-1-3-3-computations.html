3.1, 3.2, 3.3: Intro and Computations for Second Order Constant Coefficient Homogeneous ODE

Review: First Order DEs

So far we have focused on solving certain special kinds of first order DEs — namely separable equations of the form

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)\,h(y), \]

and first order linear equations of the form

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)\,y = Q(x). \]

In a first order linear equation, the dependent variable \(y\) and its derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) appear "linearly" — you won't see terms like \(y^2\), \(e^y\), or \(y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\).

Second Order Linear Differential Equations

The most general 2nd order differential equation has the form

\[ G(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'') = 0. \]

A 2nd order linear differential equation has the more specific form

\[ A(x)\,y'' + B(x)\,y' + C(x)\,y = F(x). \]

Just as in the first order case, "linear" means the dependent variable and its derivatives \(y, y', y''\) appear linearly. The coefficient functions \(A(x), B(x), C(x)\) and the source term \(F(x)\) can be nonlinear functions of the independent variable \(x\). The term \(F(x)\) on the right-hand side is called the source term and does not involve \(y, y', y''\).

Motivating Application: Mass-Spring-Dashpot

Consider a mass \(m\) attached to a spring and a dashpot (shock absorber). Let \(x(t)\) denote the displacement of the mass from its equilibrium position at time \(t\).

The dashpot converts mechanical energy into heat/sound. It exerts a resistive force proportional to the mass's speed, opposing the direction of motion, with damping constant \(c > 0\):

\[ F_R = -c\,\frac{dx}{dt}. \]

The spring follows Hooke's law: it exerts a restoring force proportional to displacement, directed back toward equilibrium, with spring constant \(k > 0\):

\[ F_S = -k\,x. \]

By Newton's second law \(ma = F\), we get

\[ m\,\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = F_R + F_S = -c\,\frac{dx}{dt} - k\,x, \]

which we rearrange as the homogeneous equation (no external forcing):

Damped Harmonic Oscillator
\[ m\,\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\,\frac{dx}{dt} + k\,x = 0. \]

If there is an external driving force \(F(t)\) (e.g. a motor or speaker), we instead have the non-homogeneous (driven) equation:

\[ m\,\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\,\frac{dx}{dt} + k\,x = F(t). \]

For electrical engineers: this is the same equation governing current \(I(t)\) in an LRC circuit:

\[ L\,\frac{d^2I}{dt^2} + R\,\frac{dI}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}\,I = F(t), \]

where \(F(t) = V'(t)\) is the derivative of the input voltage.

Principle of Superposition

Write a second order homogeneous equation in the standard form (dividing through by \(A(x)\)):

\[ y'' + p(x)\,y' + q(x)\,y = 0. \]
Superposition Principle

If \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are two solutions to a homogeneous linear equation, then any linear combination

\[ y = c_1\,y_1 + c_2\,y_2 \]

is also a solution, for any constants \(c_1, c_2\).

Why does superposition work?

Two key facts make this work:

First, derivative rules "respect linear combinations": if \(y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2\), then \(y' = c_1 y_1' + c_2 y_2'\) and \(y'' = c_1 y_1'' + c_2 y_2''\).

Second, the equation is linear and homogeneous (equals zero). Substituting \(y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2\) into the left side:

\[ \begin{aligned} &(c_1 y_1'' + c_2 y_2'') + p(x)(c_1 y_1' + c_2 y_2') + q(x)(c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2) \\ &= c_1\!\underbrace{\left(y_1'' + p(x)y_1' + q(x)y_1\right)}_{=\,0} + c_2\!\underbrace{\left(y_2'' + p(x)y_2' + q(x)y_2\right)}_{=\,0} \\ &= c_1(0) + c_2(0) = 0. \;\checkmark \end{aligned} \]

This is analogous to the fact from Math 220 (linear algebra) that the null space of a matrix is closed under linear combinations — the solution space of a homogeneous linear ODE forms a subspace/vector space.

General Solution Structure

Fact

Once you find two linearly independent solutions \(y_1(x)\) and \(y_2(x)\) to the equation \(y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0\) (provided \(p\) and \(q\) are continuous), the general solution is

\[ y(x) = c_1\,y_1(x) + c_2\,y_2(x). \]

Two functions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent when they are NOT constant multiples of each other. For example, \(y_1 = e^{2x}\) and \(y_2 = 12\,e^{2x}\) are linearly dependent (since \(y_2 = 12\,y_1\)), but \(y_1 = e^{2x}\) and \(y_2 = 10\,e^{3x}\) are linearly independent (since \(y_2/y_1 = 10\,e^x\) is not a constant).

The Euler Ansatz for Constant Coefficient DEs

For a constant coefficient homogeneous equation

\[ Ay'' + By' + Cy = 0, \]

we make an educated guess (ansatz) that solutions have the form \(y = e^{rx}\). Substituting:

\[ y = e^{rx}, \quad y' = r\,e^{rx}, \quad y'' = r^2 e^{rx}. \]

Plugging into the DE:

\[ Ar^2 e^{rx} + Br\,e^{rx} + Ce^{rx} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e^{rx}(Ar^2 + Br + C) = 0. \]

Since \(e^{rx} \neq 0\) for all \(x\), we need the characteristic polynomial to vanish:

\[ Ar^2 + Br + C = 0. \]

This is a quadratic in \(r\), and its roots determine the form of the general solution. There are three cases depending on the discriminant \(B^2 - 4AC\).

The Three Cases: Summary

For the characteristic polynomial \(Ar^2 + Br + C = 0\) with roots given by \(r_{1,2} = \frac{-B \pm \sqrt{B^2 - 4AC}}{2A}\):

Case 1 — Distinct Real Roots

If \(r_1 \neq r_2\) are real, two linearly independent solutions are

\[ y_1 = e^{r_1 x} \quad\text{and}\quad y_2 = e^{r_2 x}. \]

General solution: \(y = c_1\,e^{r_1 x} + c_2\,e^{r_2 x}\).

Case 2 — Repeated Real Root

If \(r_1 = r_2 = r_0\) is a double root, two linearly independent solutions are

\[ y_1 = e^{r_0 x} \quad\text{and}\quad y_2 = x\,e^{r_0 x}. \]

General solution: \(y = c_1\,e^{r_0 x} + c_2\,x\,e^{r_0 x}\).

Case 3 — Complex Conjugate Roots

If \(r_{1,2} = a \pm bi\) with \(b \neq 0\), two linearly independent real-valued solutions are

\[ y_1 = e^{ax}\cos(bx) \quad\text{and}\quad y_2 = e^{ax}\sin(bx). \]

General solution: \(y = c_1\,e^{ax}\cos(bx) + c_2\,e^{ax}\sin(bx)\).

Note: since the characteristic polynomial has real coefficients, any non-real roots must come in conjugate pairs \(a + bi\) and \(a - bi\). This is a consequence of the quadratic formula.

Examples: Distinct Real Roots

Example

Find the general solution to \(y'' + 5y' + 6y = 0\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 + 5r + 6 = 0\). Factoring: \((r+2)(r+3) = 0\), so \(r = -2, -3\).

Two linearly independent solutions: \(y_1 = e^{-2x}\) and \(y_2 = e^{-3x}\).

\[ \boxed{y = c_1\,e^{-2x} + c_2\,e^{-3x}.} \]
Example

Find the general solution to \(y'' - 4y = 0\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 - 4 = 0\), so \((r+2)(r-2) = 0\) and \(r = \pm 2\).

\[ \boxed{y = c_1\,e^{2x} + c_2\,e^{-2x}.} \]

Example: Repeated Root

Example

Find the general solution to \(y'' - 4y' + 4y = 0\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0\), which factors as \((r-2)^2 = 0\). So \(r = 2\) is a double root.

Two linearly independent solutions: \(y_1 = e^{2x}\) and \(y_2 = x\,e^{2x}\).

\[ \boxed{y = c_1\,e^{2x} + c_2\,x\,e^{2x}.} \]

Example: Complex Conjugate Roots

Example

Find the general solution to \(y'' - 4y' + 13y = 0\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 - 4r + 13 = 0\). Completing the square:

\[ r^2 - 4r + 4 + 9 = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (r-2)^2 = -9 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r - 2 = \pm 3i. \]

So \(r = 2 \pm 3i\), giving \(a = 2\) and \(b = 3\).

\[ \boxed{y = c_1\,e^{2x}\cos(3x) + c_2\,e^{2x}\sin(3x).} \]

Initial Conditions for Second Order IVPs

Since the general solution has two free parameters \(c_1, c_2\), knowing only \(y(0) = y_0\) is not enough. For second order DEs, a complete initial value problem specifies both the initial "position" and "velocity":

Second Order IVP
\[ \begin{cases} Ay'' + By' + Cy = 0, \\ y(0) = y_0, \\ y'(0) = v_0. \end{cases} \]

These two conditions uniquely determine \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).

IVP Examples

Example — Distinct Roots IVP

Solve \(y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0\), \(\;y(0) = 2\), \(\;y'(0) = 5\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 - 5r + 6 = (r-2)(r-3) = 0\), so \(r = 2, 3\).

General solution: \(y = c_1\,e^{2x} + c_2\,e^{3x}\). Then \(y' = 2c_1\,e^{2x} + 3c_2\,e^{3x}\).

Applying initial conditions at \(x = 0\):

\[ \begin{cases} c_1 + c_2 = 2 \\ 2c_1 + 3c_2 = 5 \end{cases} \]

Subtracting twice the first equation from the second: \(c_2 = 1\), and then \(c_1 = 1\).

\[ \boxed{y = e^{2x} + e^{3x}.} \]
Example — Complex Roots IVP

Solve \(y'' - 10y' + 29y = 0\), \(\;y(0) = 1\), \(\;y'(0) = 4\).

Characteristic polynomial: \(r^2 - 10r + 29 = 0\). Completing the square:

\[ (r - 5)^2 + 4 = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r = 5 \pm 2i. \]

General solution: \(y = c_1\,e^{5x}\cos(2x) + c_2\,e^{5x}\sin(2x)\).

Differentiating (product rule):

\[ y' = 5c_1\,e^{5x}\cos(2x) - 2c_1\,e^{5x}\sin(2x) + 5c_2\,e^{5x}\sin(2x) + 2c_2\,e^{5x}\cos(2x). \]

At \(x = 0\): \(\cos(0) = 1\), \(\sin(0) = 0\), \(e^0 = 1\), so:

\[ \begin{cases} y(0) = c_1 = 1, \\ y'(0) = 5c_1 + 2c_2 = 4. \end{cases} \]

From the first equation \(c_1 = 1\). Substituting: \(5 + 2c_2 = 4\), so \(c_2 = -\tfrac{1}{2}\).

\[ \boxed{y = e^{5x}\cos(2x) - \tfrac{1}{2}\,e^{5x}\sin(2x).} \]

Where Do the Cosines and Sines Come From?

When the characteristic roots are \(r_{1,2} = a \pm bi\), the "naive" solutions would be the complex-valued functions \(y_1 = e^{(a+bi)x}\) and \(y_2 = e^{(a-bi)x}\). But we want real-valued solutions.

Euler's Formula via Power Series

Recall from calculus the power series (valid for all real \(x\)):

\[ e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!}, \qquad \cos(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}, \qquad \sin(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}. \]

These series converge for complex inputs too, so we define \(e^z := \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{n!}\) for all complex \(z\). Substituting \(z = ibx\) and using the fact that powers of \(i\) cycle as \(i^0 = 1,\; i^1 = i,\; i^2 = -1,\; i^3 = -i,\; i^4 = 1, \ldots\), we can separate the series into real and imaginary parts:

\[ e^{ibx} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(ibx)^n}{n!} = \underbrace{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n(bx)^{2n}}{(2n)!}}_{\cos(bx)} + i\underbrace{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n(bx)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}}_{\sin(bx)}. \]

This gives Euler's formula:

Euler's Formula
\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta. \]

Therefore:

\[ e^{(a+bi)x} = e^{ax}\,e^{ibx} = e^{ax}\bigl(\cos(bx) + i\sin(bx)\bigr). \]

Extracting Real Solutions

Since the ODE has real coefficients, the real and imaginary parts of any complex solution each separately satisfy the ODE. So we take:

\[ y_1 = \operatorname{Re}\!\left(e^{(a+bi)x}\right) = e^{ax}\cos(bx), \qquad y_2 = \operatorname{Im}\!\left(e^{(a+bi)x}\right) = e^{ax}\sin(bx). \]

These are two linearly independent real-valued solutions, and the general solution via superposition is \(y = c_1\,e^{ax}\cos(bx) + c_2\,e^{ax}\sin(bx)\).

Where Does the Extra \(x\) Come From for Double Roots?

Define the differential operator \(P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right) = A\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + B\frac{d}{dx} + C\), so the characteristic polynomial is \(P(r) = Ar^2 + Br + C\). A key fact is:

\[ P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right) e^{rx} = P(r)\,e^{rx}. \]

We want to show that if \(r_0\) is a double root, then \(y = x\,e^{r_0 x}\) is also a solution. The trick is to notice that

\[ x\,e^{r_0 x} = \frac{d}{dr}\,e^{rx}\bigg|_{r=r_0} \]

since differentiating \(e^{rx}\) with respect to \(r\) (treating \(x\) as a constant) gives \(x\,e^{rx}\). Therefore:

\[ P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right)(x\,e^{r_0 x}) = \frac{d}{dr}\left[P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right)e^{rx}\right]\bigg|_{r=r_0} = \frac{d}{dr}\left[P(r)\,e^{rx}\right]\bigg|_{r=r_0}. \]

Here we used Clairaut's theorem (from Math 230/231) to switch the order of the \(\frac{d}{dx}\) and \(\frac{d}{dr}\) derivatives. Applying the product rule in \(r\):

\[ = P'(r)\,e^{rx} + P(r)\,x\,e^{rx}\bigg|_{r=r_0} = P'(r_0)\,e^{r_0 x} + P(r_0)\,x\,e^{r_0 x}. \]

If \(r_0\) is a double root of \(P(r)\), then \(P(r) = A(r - r_0)^2\), so both \(P(r_0) = 0\) and \(P'(r_0) = 0\). Therefore:

\[ P\!\left(\frac{d}{dx}\right)(x\,e^{r_0 x}) = 0, \]

confirming that \(x\,e^{r_0 x}\) is indeed a solution.

Important

The "extra \(x\)" trick is specific to double roots. For instance, for \(y'' + 5y' + 6y = 0\) with distinct roots \(r = -2, -3\), the general solution is \(y = c_1 e^{-2x} + c_2 e^{-3x}\). Neither \(x\,e^{-2x}\) nor \(x\,e^{-3x}\) are solutions in this case!

Summary

Key Takeaways

To solve \(Ay'' + By' + Cy = 0\):

(1) Form the characteristic polynomial \(Ar^2 + Br + C = 0\) and find its roots. (2) Use the appropriate case (distinct real, repeated real, or complex conjugate) to write down two linearly independent solutions. (3) The general solution is \(y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2\) by superposition. (4) For an IVP, use both initial conditions \(y(0) = y_0\) and \(y'(0) = v_0\) to solve for \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).

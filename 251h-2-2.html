Section 2.2: Equilibrium Solutions and Stability

Background: Population Models (Section 2.1)

In Section 2.1, we introduced two population models:

  • Exponential growth: \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = kP\)
  • Logistic growth: \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = kP(M - P)\), where \(M\) is the carrying capacity.

We now develop tools to understand the long-term behavior of solutions to these (and other) equations without solving them explicitly.

Equilibrium Solutions

Definition An equilibrium solution (also called a fixed point or constant solution) of the differential equation \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = F(x, y) \] is a solution of the form \(y = \text{const}\).

Graphically, an equilibrium solution is a horizontal line in the \(xy\)-plane. If the system starts at an equilibrium value, it stays there forever.

Autonomous Differential Equations

Definition An autonomous differential equation does not explicitly involve the independent variable. It has the form \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(y). \] Other parameters may appear, but \(x\) itself does not appear anywhere on the right-hand side.

Equilibrium Solutions of Autonomous Equations

For an autonomous equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(y)\), the equilibrium solutions correspond exactly to the zeros of \(f(y)\).

Indeed, if \(f(a) = 0\), then the constant function \(y = a\) solves the DE. We can verify: if \(y = a\) for all \(x\), then \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0\), and substituting gives

\[ 0 \stackrel{?}{=} f(a) = 0. \quad\checkmark \]

Stability of Equilibrium Solutions

Definition — Stable Equilibrium An equilibrium solution \(y^*\) of \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(y)\) is stable if for all initial values \(y(0)\) close enough to \(y^*\), the solution \(y(x)\) satisfies \[ \lim_{x \to \infty} y(x) = y^*. \] A stable equilibrium "attracts" nearby solutions.

For \(y^*\) to be stable, we need:

  • \(f(y) > 0\) for all \(y\) slightly less than \(y^*\) (solutions below \(y^*\) are increasing toward it), and
  • \(f(y) < 0\) for all \(y\) slightly greater than \(y^*\) (solutions above \(y^*\) are decreasing toward it).
Definition — Unstable Equilibrium If the opposite holds — \(f(y) < 0\) for \(y\) slightly less than \(y^*\) and \(f(y) > 0\) for \(y\) slightly greater than \(y^*\) — then \(y^*\) is an unstable equilibrium. Nearby solutions are "pushed away."
Definition — Semistable Equilibrium If \(f(y)\) has the same sign on both sides of \(y^*\) (attracting from one side, repelling from the other), then \(y^*\) is semistable.

You can summarize this information on a phase line: a number line for \(y\) with equilibrium points marked, and arrows indicating the direction of motion (increasing or decreasing) in each interval.

Example 1

Problem Find and classify the equilibrium solutions to \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 4 - y^2. \]

Solution

Find equilibria: Set \(f(y) = 4 - y^2 = 0\):

\[ 4 - y^2 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; (2+y)(2-y) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; y = -2 \text{ or } y = 2. \]

Sign analysis of \(f(y) = (2+y)(2-y)\):

Interval \(y < -2\) \(-2 < y < 2\) \(y > 2\)
Sign of \(f(y)\) \((-)(+) < 0\) \((+)(+) > 0\) \((+)(-) < 0\)
Solutions are decreasing increasing decreasing

Phase line:

\[ \xleftarrow{\quad} \underset{-2}{\circ} \xrightarrow{\quad} \underset{2}{\bullet} \xleftarrow{\quad} \]

Classification:

  • \(y = 2\) is stable: \(f > 0\) below and \(f < 0\) above — nearby solutions are attracted to it.
  • \(y = -2\) is unstable: \(f < 0\) below and \(f > 0\) above — nearby solutions are repelled from it.

Putting It All Together: Qualitative Analysis

For an autonomous DE \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(y)\) (assuming \(f'\) is continuous), the zeros of \(f(y)\) give the equilibrium solutions, and sign analysis tells us the increasing/decreasing behavior of non-equilibrium solutions.

This approach — obtaining information about solutions without computing their exact formulas — is called qualitative analysis. It is a quick and powerful way to understand the general behavior of solutions.

Solutions will either veer off toward \(\pm\infty\), or their limit as \(x \to \infty\) will be a stable equilibrium value (approached asymptotically, never reached in finite time). Solutions can also approach the "stable side" of a semistable equilibrium.

Example 2

Problem Find and classify the equilibrium solutions to \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 4y^2 - y^4. \]

Solution

Find equilibria: Factor \(f(y) = 4y^2 - y^4 = y^2(4 - y^2) = y^2(2+y)(2-y)\). Setting this to zero:

\[ y = 0, \quad y = -2, \quad y = 2. \]

Sign analysis of \(f(y) = y^2(2+y)(2-y)\):

Interval \(y < -2\) \(-2 < y < 0\) \(0 < y < 2\) \(y > 2\)
Sign of \(f(y)\) \((+)(-)(+) < 0\) \((+)(+)(+) > 0\) \((+)(+)(+) > 0\) \((+)(+)(-) < 0\)
Solutions are decreasing increasing increasing decreasing

Phase line:

\[ \xleftarrow{\quad} \underset{-2}{\circ} \xrightarrow{\quad} \underset{0}{\odot} \xrightarrow{\quad} \underset{2}{\bullet} \xleftarrow{\quad} \]

Classification:

  • \(y = -2\) is unstable: \(f < 0\) below, \(f > 0\) above.
  • \(y = 0\) is semistable: \(f > 0\) on both sides (solutions increase toward and away from it on different sides — here, solutions increase on both sides, so it's attracting from the left and repelling to the right from the perspective of approaching it, but since \(f>0\) on both sides, solutions pass through; it attracts from below and repels above).
  • \(y = 2\) is stable: \(f > 0\) below, \(f < 0\) above.

Application: Falling Object with Air Resistance

Consider an object falling under gravity with air resistance proportional to velocity. By Newton's second law (\(ma = F\)):

\[ m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - kv, \]

where \(m > 0\) is mass, \(g > 0\) is gravitational acceleration, \(k > 0\) is the drag coefficient, and \(v(t)\) is the downward velocity.

Physical setup
  • When \(v > 0\) (falling down): gravity pulls down (\(mg\)), air resistance pushes up (\(-kv\)).
  • When \(v < 0\) (moving up): both gravity and air resistance point downward.

Equilibrium: Set \(mg - kv = 0\), giving \(v = \dfrac{mg}{k}\).

Phase line analysis: Writing \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = mg - kv\) as a function of \(v\), this is linear with negative slope (\(-k\)), so:

  • For \(v < \dfrac{mg}{k}\): \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} > 0\) (velocity increasing).
  • For \(v > \dfrac{mg}{k}\): \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} < 0\) (velocity decreasing).

Therefore \(v = \dfrac{mg}{k}\) is a stable equilibrium. This is the terminal velocity — all solutions approach it as \(t \to \infty\), regardless of the initial velocity.

Application: Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's law of cooling states that the rate of change of an object's temperature is proportional to the temperature difference between the object and its surroundings. If the surroundings remain at constant temperature \(T_0\):

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = k(T_0 - T), \qquad k > 0. \]

Equilibrium: Set \(k(T_0 - T) = 0\), giving \(T = T_0\).

Stability: Writing the right side as \(kT_0 - kT\), this is a linear function of \(T\) with negative slope \(-k\). So:

  • For \(T < T_0\): \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} > 0\) (object warms up).
  • For \(T > T_0\): \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} < 0\) (object cools down).

Therefore \(T = T_0\) is stable. All solutions approach the ambient temperature. The general solution is \(T(t) = T_0 + Ce^{-kt}\), confirming that \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} T(t) = T_0\).

Application: Logistic Population Model

The logistic model is

\[ \frac{dP}{dt} = kP(M - P), \qquad k > 0,\; M > 0. \]

Equilibria: Set \(kP(M-P) = 0\), giving \(P = 0\) and \(P = M\).

Sign analysis: \(f(P) = kP(M-P)\) is a downward-opening parabola (leading coefficient \(-k < 0\)) with zeros at \(P = 0\) and \(P = M\):

Interval \(P < 0\) \(0 < P < M\) \(P > M\)
Sign of \(f(P)\) \(< 0\) \(> 0\) \(< 0\)

Classification:

  • \(P = 0\) is unstable: any positive initial population grows away from zero.
  • \(P = M\) is stable: populations are attracted to the carrying capacity.

Bifurcations

Many models contain physical or biological parameters. When a parameter is varied, the number and/or type of equilibria may change.

Definition A bifurcation point is a parameter value at which a qualitative change in the behavior of equilibrium solutions occurs (equilibria appear, disappear, or change stability).

A bifurcation diagram plots the equilibrium values of the dependent variable against the parameter. It is essentially "all the phase lines for different parameter values stacked horizontally." Stable equilibria are drawn as solid curves and unstable equilibria as dashed curves.

General Procedure

For the autonomous equation with parameter \(r\),

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, r), \]
  1. Set \(f(x, r) = 0\) and solve for \(x\) in terms of \(r\). Sketch these curves in the \(rx\)-plane — these are the equilibrium curves.
  2. The equilibrium curves divide the \(rx\)-plane into regions. Since \(f\) is continuous, \(f\) has constant sign in each region. Check one test point per region to determine the sign.
  3. Use the signs (arrows pointing up where \(f > 0\), down where \(f < 0\)) to classify each equilibrium branch as stable, unstable, or semistable.
  4. Identify bifurcation points: parameter values where the number or type of equilibria changes.

Example 3 — Transcritical Bifurcation

Problem Consider \[ \frac{dx}{dt} = rx - x^2, \] where \(r\) is a parameter. Sketch the phase lines for \(r = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2\). At what value of \(r\) does a bifurcation occur? Describe the qualitative change and sketch the bifurcation diagram.

Solution

Find equilibria: \(f(x,r) = rx - x^2 = x(r - x) = 0\), giving \(x = 0\) and \(x = r\).

Phase lines for specific \(r\) values:

\(r = -2\): \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = -2x - x^2 = -x(2+x)\). Equilibria at \(x = -2\) and \(x = 0\). Sign analysis: \((-)(+)(-)=(-)\) for \(x < -2\), \((+)(-)=(+)\) for \(-2 < x < 0\), \((-)(-)=(-)\) for \(x > 0\). So \(x = -2\) is unstable, \(x = 0\) is stable.

\(r = -1\): Equilibria at \(x = -1\) and \(x = 0\). Same pattern: \(x = -1\) is unstable, \(x = 0\) is stable.

\(r = 0\): \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = -x^2\). Only equilibrium at \(x = 0\). Since \(-x^2 \le 0\) everywhere (negative on both sides of 0), \(x = 0\) is semistable.

\(r = 1\): Equilibria at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\). Sign analysis shows \(x = 0\) is unstable, \(x = 1\) is stable.

\(r = 2\): Equilibria at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 2\). Again \(x = 0\) is unstable, \(x = 2\) is stable.

Bifurcation occurs at \(r = 0\).

Description of the bifurcation
  • For \(r < 0\): There is an unstable equilibrium at \(x = r\) (to the left of the origin) and a stable equilibrium at \(x = 0\).
  • As \(r \to 0\): The unstable equilibrium at \(x = r\) moves toward \(x = 0\).
  • At \(r = 0\): The two equilibria collide at \(x = 0\), forming a single semistable equilibrium.
  • For \(r > 0\): Now \(x = 0\) is unstable, and the new equilibrium at \(x = r\) (to the right of the origin) is stable. The stabilities have swapped.
This is called a transcritical bifurcation — two equilibrium branches exchange stability as they pass through each other.

Bifurcation diagram: In the \(rx\)-plane, the two equilibrium curves are \(x = 0\) (horizontal line) and \(x = r\) (diagonal line). They cross at the origin. For \(r < 0\), the \(x = 0\) branch is stable (solid) and \(x = r\) is unstable (dashed). For \(r > 0\), they switch.

Example 4 — Harvesting and Saddle-Node Bifurcation

Problem Consider a fish population modeled by \[ \frac{dP}{dt} = P(4 - P) - h, \] where \(k = 1\), \(M = 4\) (logistic model), and \(h\) represents constant harvesting of \(h\) fish per year. Draw a bifurcation diagram and determine which values of \(h\) are sustainable.

Solution

Find equilibria: Set \(P(4 - P) - h = 0\), i.e., \(h = P(4 - P) = -P^2 + 4P\).

Completing the square:

\[ h = -(P^2 - 4P) = -(P^2 - 4P + 4 - 4) = -(P-2)^2 + 4. \]

This is a left-opening parabola in the \(hP\)-plane with vertex at \((h, P) = (4, 2)\).

Solving for \(P\) in terms of \(h\): from \(h - 4 = -(P-2)^2\), we get

\[ P = 2 \pm \sqrt{4 - h}. \]

This requires \(h \le 4\) for real solutions.

Sign analysis:

  • Inside the parabola (\(h < P(4-P)\), i.e., \(h < -(P-2)^2 + 4\)): \(f > 0\), solutions increase.
  • Outside the parabola (\(h > P(4-P)\)): \(f < 0\), solutions decrease.

The upper branch \(P = 2 + \sqrt{4-h}\) is stable (arrows point toward it from both sides) and the lower branch \(P = 2 - \sqrt{4-h}\) is unstable.

Bifurcation point: \(h = 4\).

Classification of harvesting rates
  • For \(0 < h < 4\): Two equilibrium solutions exist (one stable, one unstable). Fishing is sustainable as long as the initial population \(P(0)\) is above the unstable equilibrium, i.e., \(P(0) > 2 - \sqrt{4 - h}\). The population will approach the stable equilibrium \(P = 2 + \sqrt{4-h}\).
  • At \(h = 4\): The two equilibria collide to form a single semistable equilibrium at \(P = 2\). This is the threshold — a saddle-node bifurcation.
  • For \(h > 4\): No equilibrium solutions exist. \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} < 0\) for all \(P\), so the population always decreases — unsustainable harvesting leads to extinction regardless of the initial population.

Example 5 — Pitchfork Bifurcations

Problem Analyze the bifurcation diagrams for the equations \[ \text{(i)}\;\; \frac{dx}{dt} = rx - x^3 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \text{(ii)}\;\; \frac{dx}{dt} = rx + x^3. \]

(i) Supercritical Pitchfork: \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = rx - x^3\)

Equilibria: \(x(r - x^2) = 0\), so \(x = 0\) always, and \(x = \pm\sqrt{r}\) when \(r > 0\).

For \(r \le 0\): Only \(x = 0\). Since \(f(x) = x(r - x^2)\) and \(r - x^2 < 0\) for all \(x \ne 0\) when \(r \le 0\), the sign of \(f\) is opposite to the sign of \(x\). So \(x = 0\) is stable.

For \(r = 0\): \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = -x^3\). Only equilibrium at \(x=0\), which is stable (solutions decrease for \(x>0\), increase for \(x<0\)).

For \(r > 0\): Three equilibria: \(x = -\sqrt{r}\), \(x = 0\), \(x = \sqrt{r}\). Sign analysis shows \(x = 0\) becomes unstable, while \(x = \pm\sqrt{r}\) are both stable.

Bifurcation at \(r = 0\): A single stable equilibrium splits into an unstable one flanked by two new stable ones. This is the supercritical pitchfork bifurcation — considered "safe" because the system transitions smoothly to nearby stable states.

(ii) Subcritical Pitchfork: \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = rx + x^3\)

Equilibria: \(x(r + x^2) = 0\), so \(x = 0\) always, and \(x = \pm\sqrt{-r}\) when \(r < 0\).

For \(r < 0\): Three equilibria: \(x = 0\) is stable, and \(x = \pm\sqrt{-r}\) are both unstable.

For \(r \ge 0\): Only \(x = 0\), which is unstable.

Bifurcation at \(r = 0\): Two unstable equilibria collapse into the origin, causing the single remaining equilibrium to become unstable. This is the subcritical pitchfork bifurcation — considered "dangerous" because the system loses all stable equilibria and solutions may blow up.

Physical example Pitchfork bifurcations arise in structural mechanics. A toy model: let \(x(t)\) represent the horizontal deviation at the top of a skyscraper, and \(r\) an "age parameter" reflecting wear and tear. As \(r\) increases through zero, the upright equilibrium can become unstable.

Summary of Bifurcation Types

Bifurcation Normal Form What Happens
Saddle-node \(\dot{x} = r - x^2\) Two equilibria (one stable, one unstable) collide and annihilate
Transcritical \(\dot{x} = rx - x^2\) Two equilibria exchange stability as they pass through each other
Supercritical pitchfork \(\dot{x} = rx - x^3\) One stable equilibrium becomes unstable; two new stable ones appear ("safe")
Subcritical pitchfork \(\dot{x} = rx + x^3\) Two unstable equilibria collapse into the origin, which becomes unstable ("dangerous")
